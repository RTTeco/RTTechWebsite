A Giant in the Gym

    Who is about twenty feet high and can dunk without jumping? Who can make a whole gymnasium erupt with laughter without saying a word? The name Michael Cooper should come to mind. Michael Cooper came to RTHS on Monday, September 20 to show the student body that the arts are still very much alive.
    Mr. Cooper is a mime from Maine. Cooper leaves Maine for one to two weeks every month to perform across the United States. He then goes back home and stays home for one to two weeks to make more props. He does this all year, but tries to get most of the summer off so he can spend some time with his family. Mr. Cooper has two sons and a lovely wife back home in Maine.
    Cooper was 19 years old when he first decided to become a mime. He was watching T.V one afternoon and was instantly inspired. In fact, he was so inspired that he left college, and went off to a professional acting school in Maine. His family and friends thought that he was crazy, but provided much encouragement. When he finished school there, he decided to take his career one step further. Cooper went all of the way to Paris, France to a special, professional acting school. As we saw Monday in our gymnasium, all of these acting lessons paid off. With all of his determination, he has made his life a success. Although we never did ask Cooper what kind of living he makes, he informed us that he makes a very good one. Despite his great success as a mime, he went back to Maine to finish college and received his degree.
    Michael Cooper doesn’t only act, he is also somewhat of an artist. He creates all of his own props out of papier mache, cloth, and glue. Some of his masks are in so much detail that they take him up to eighty hours to finish! The patience that this man must have, some of us will never experience.
    Cooper encourages everyone to follow their dreams because you never know what might come of them. If you see a twenty foot man roaming around the area, don’t be frightened. It is probably just Michael Cooper living his dream.

Why Alphabetize?

    In the history of Richardton High School, we have never seen such a school wide change as the new locker situation. For years, according to Charlotte Zimmerman, the school secretary, the issue of alphabetized lockers was talked about, but only implemented with new and larger changes. It seems that along with the fact that alphabetization is easier for Char to record, the administration felt it was necessary that the separation of favorites must be made. Thus came… alphabetized lockers.
    As one student put it, it was okay, but felt it would lead to more stringent locker policies. However, for the majority of the people we spoke with, it was suggested that the new system is okay.
    A senior, Mike Staudinger, said, "Since it is the last year for us with the lockers, we should be by close friends. If necessary, the underclassmen can be alphabetized, but not seniors; (choosing who you’re by) is like a senior privilege." In agreement with Michael, Lee Hutchinson believes that "Seniors (should) pick out what type of lockers they use."
    From the perspective of a newcomer, Jenni Schank believes it is ok, but has a complaint with locker spacing. She would not change the situation back. However, a sophomore, Matt Becker, feels that the locker situation in its entirety is not the most intelligent thing that the administration has done. He feels that not being able to have same sex conversations is a violation, on the administration’s part, against personal freedom. He also suggests that if so be, he would change his name.
    It seems to us that, for the majority of the student body, it doesn’t appear to have a dramatic toll on their social life, and, for the most part, the students don’t mind. Char was quoted with also saying she feels that everyone was cheated equally.
    In answer to everyone’s question on "why" it was done, Mr. Quintus reasoned that this policy was done to make Char’s work easier, as far as knowing "who lives where" when it came to lockers. Mr. Gengler boldly stated, "Guys are okay, let’s face it, but the girls seem to need to be by each other to live," an issue that is clearly visible here at RTHS.
