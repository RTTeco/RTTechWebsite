Schnell Ranch

Nature Trail Narrative

 

Marker 1 The gray-green bush about 100 feet to your right is called silver buffalo berry. In some years, it is a prolific producer of red (and sometimes yellow and purple) fruits used by wildlife for food, and often by people in making jams and jellies.
Marker 2 Five feet south of this post, in May and early June, you will see quite a few wild onion plants about six inches high. If you eat some wild onion bulbs, they will leave you with a very pungent, long lasting odor.

Between this post and the next, you may want to take the opportunity to sneak closer to the second pond, which is on your right beyond the oak trees. Wood ducks use the artificial nest boxes, of both plastic and wood, on both ponds. They may produce as many as one brood a year per box for this area. A limiting factor to many birds in our area is a lack of good nesting sites.

The green brush in the small draw ahead is commonly called buckbrush, or western snowberry. The pink flowered shrubs are good forage for livestock and deer, and the white berries, which last all winter, are important to upland birds such as sharp-tailed grouse.
Marker 3 Around this marker and to the north you can see a low, thick carpet of a fine, short grass named blue grama. It is highly nutritious for livestock, and the bison which used to populate the plains.

Five feet away to the right are wavy-leaf thistles, a native thistle to North Dakota. This thistle is practically worthless as forage and is considered an invader. The seed heads are frequently favored by horses, even when an abundance of other forage is available.

The plants about a foot and a half tall with clusters of tiny white flowers, are called western yarrow. Yarrow or related species are said to have been used by the Greeks to cure wounds, and by the American Indians as a mild laxative. Even in recent years yarrow has been on the pharmacist’s list of preferred medicinal plants as a stimulant and tonic.

The tall bluish grasses are western wheatgrass. It is very common in the Northern Great Plains, palatable to livestock, and quite drought resistant.
Marker 4 Another tall blue-green grass to the north and east of this marker is called needle and thread grass. For many people it evokes memories of blazing hot July afternoons of childhood fun. Groups of children will pull the six-inch awls and attached seed head out of the plant, gathering while warily eyeing on another. When an adequate arsenal is gathered, someone will suddenly attack…The battle begins! Miniature spears fly for excited minutes…attacks and retreats surge across the prairie…in the end, the loser can look like a porcupine. (Ironically, there is another species of needle grass called porcupine grass.) You may want to try throwing some needle grass "spears" yourself, but don’t throw them at an unwilling participant, and never at anyone’s face! These special sticking properties of the seed heads are the mechanism by which the grass spreads.

The low prickly bush on your way to the next marker is called prickly currant or Missouri gooseberry.

Prairie sand grass is the yellow clumps of grass. This yellow grass is a sign of sandy soil. Notice how loose and light the soils are around the area where the grass grows. Rodents also like to dig in this loose soil.

Across the stream another major drainage branches of to the west.
Marker 5 Take a few moments and sit on the rock shelf.

There is American licorice growing at your feet and wild grape below the drop-off on the other side of the path. Prickly wild roses with their serrated leaves grow across the shelf as well as juneberry and chokecherry.

This stand of quaking aspen trees is uncommon for this part of the state. Something about this location, possibly an underground seep of water, which the trees can access, has allowed aspens to survive few places in southwestern North Dakota. The current suspension of livestock grazing is allowing for the survival of many new aspen sprouts, which livestock like to eat.

Notice the "bonsai-like" or stunted aspen trees growing on the shallow soil on the rock. The stunted trees have very little nutrition or moisture available in the shallow soil. These are the limiting factors to the growth of trees on this rock shelf.

Note dark patches on some of the leaves of other normal sized aspens. These are the "dairy farms" of certain ants! If you look closely, you will see huge numbers of aphids grouped have herded together on the leaves. They are busily sucking the aspen sap, getting vital nutrients from it, and passing the concentrated sap out of their bodies, which the ants "harvest". The ants can use this aphid "honey" as food. You will often find sticky little blobs of this sap all over your car after you have parked under a tree.

On your right are some American elm among the predominant bur oak and ash. They are a majestic native tree, unfortunately threatened by (the introduced) Dutch elm disease.
Marker 6 The stream splits again, with a branch going to the southwest, with the main one to the south.

Notice the common light purple daisy-like flower with a dark center around this marker. The roots of black sampson, otherwise called the purple coneflower, were used buy American Indians as dentists use Novocaine today, as well as for burns, mumps, and for distemper treatment in horses. In some areas it is still being collected and sold as a medicinal herb. Modern medicine still uses extracts from the roots for healing wounds and curing sore throat.

About two-thirds of your way to marker number 7, in a sparse area, you can see a number of white prairie-clover plants. They are tasty and nutritious to grazing animals, and important to the prairie ecosystem, since white prairie-clover fixes nitrogen, or in other words, makes gaseous nitrogen from the air available in a solid nitrate from that other plants can use to grow more vigorously.

An especially intriguing denizen or occupant of bare, dry sandy areas might be found if you take a few minutes and look closely. Especially good sandy areas may be found if you take a detour off of the trial and look below the little "drop-offs" on the sandy slope above you. If you find and odd, unexpected cone shaped depression in fine sand about an inch and a half deep, and two inches across, you might try dropping an ant into it and watching what happens. As the ant tries to climb out of the cone shaped hole, it will dislodge sand, which will fall to the bottom of the hole. Soon, you may see flicks of sand hitting the ant. Both of the ant’s feet, and especially the hard flung little flicks of sand will disturb the sand of the cone, which is at it’s "angle of repose" or limit of steepness at which it can stay without moving. When the sand is disturbed it will move to the bottom of the hole where an ant lion is waiting with huge open jaws. If the ant lion is hungry, it will grab the ant, pierce its body with fangs, and suck all of its bodily fluids out, and fling the empty husk of an ant out of the hole. Then it will flick more sand out until it is again at its angle of repose, and [Snidely Whiplash-like] await another victim! On the scale of an ant, it is a sad story, but it is the only way that the ant lion, the larvae of another insect, has found to live. Please don’t try to dig in the hole to see what the ant lion looks like, they are fast and impossible to catch.
Marker 7 You can see the effects of a late 1980’s in the large number of dead trees in the drainage and dead buffaloberries on the slope.

The short fine grass at the end of the point and above the short drop-off behind you is thread leaf sedge. It lives on drier sites, and is one of the first plants to green up in the spring.
Marker 8 To your right, the pooled up water (in late summer may be covered completely by duckweed) is caused by deadfall trees which catch smaller vegetation forming natural dams that provide a microenvironment for different plant and animals. (Microenvironment means an environment or set of conditions that are different from the most common environment or conditions predominating in area.)

Around this marker, in June, you may notice many white foamy globs at the base of plant sap by insects, and used as their homes.

The red osier dogwood in the swampy area by the stream is an uncommon plant around this part of the state. It is much more common in cooler, wetter climates.
Marker 9 The largest tree behind and to your left is one of the largest bur oaks on the property and is certainly the most attractive. It may be four hundred years old or more. Bur oaks are also not common on the prairie lands in this area.
Marker 10 To your right is what remains of possibly the largest peach leaf willow on the property. There are many young trees sprouting in this area.
Marker 11 On this south facing slope, which is more exposed to the sun, you will find more dead trees than in other moister microenvironments.

If you look closely under the plum tree, you could possibly see an abandoned fox hole.
Marker 12 Thirty feet ahead and to the right are serviceberry or juneberry and American plum bushes. They are both important to wildlife, and were to American Indians and the pioneers. These are remarkable for serviceberry stems, reaching almost twenty feet high and three inches thick.

Nine feet left of the marker you can find the short, yet elegant white milkwort. It grows from four to sixteen inches tall, and in June and July, has white flowers which bloom from the bottom up. The flowerhead can have the appearance of a spearpoint from a distance because of this method of flowering. White milkwort and some related species are economically important for medicinal purposes. A substance extracted from the roots of the milkworts has been used to treat respiratory ailments. Previously, American Indians and later settlers used the extract as an antidote for snakebite.
Marker 13 Here under the forest canopy, there is a sheltered, shaded, more moist environment protected from much of the drying effects of the wind and sun. This provides a microclimate where forest plants, non-typical of our prairies, can survive. (Microclimate is much like microenvironment, but is more specific by referring only to the small scale conditions in the environment relating to climate. Microclimate refers to climate that on a small scale is different from the overall climate of an area.) Take time to look; there are interesting plants on the forest floor. Another indication of the moist environment are the shelf fungi and the and the odd bulbous fungi projecting down from the dead chokecherry tree overhead, and from the oak 80 feet ahead.
Marker 14 Here the trail crosses an intermittent stream – one that flows only after it rains. Downstream a spring provides enough water to form a "live" stream.

The effects of a drought in the late 1980’s can be seen in this draw. Trees below the spring had ample supplies of water; those above the spring were restricted to moisture from precipitation and snowmelt. The death of trees in a common occurrence in the lifecycle of woody draws. Unfortunately, in much of the west, management has skewed the character of the native biota (or living plants and animals) a great deal. In some places, the only trees in woody draws are quite old. Grazing is so continuous and heavy in those locations, that young trees are eaten and killed. Trees are not usually the favorite forage of livestock, but they will eat them if they don’t have enough palatable grasses and forbs.

In the more marginal habitats, woody vegetation has disappeared. We are trying to foster a new generation of trees on the Recreation Area by suspending grazing for while. You can see the results in numerous young trees populating many of the drainages.

Marker 16 After being in the draw for awhile, when coming out on the top of the little hill above the draw, you may feel the change in microclimate, of local climate. It will likely feel drier and windier on the prairie landscape above the woodlands. Plants and animals feel the effects of this also. Prairie plants have to be tougher and more flexible compared to forest plants. They trade off the potential for the lush showy growth of the forest plants for the ability to survive in the harsher prairie habitat more typical of this part of the world.
Marker 17 Young Man’s Butte is the prominent butte to the south-south east. It is the highest landscape point in Stark County. It was an important landmark to early travelers, and marked the general area containing springs fed by the tableland above the Russian Springs Escarpment; a discontinuous, possible glacial feature from an earlier age. This feature can be followed faintly across much of the southwestern part of the state.

The yellow sweetclover, becoming thick at this point, is an introduced species, which is quite valuable for livestock forage, and by general acclaim makes the best honey.
Marker 18 Look around this field which is full of prairie coneflowers. There are two known varieties; yellow pealed and those with reddish brown and yellow petals. Most of the coneflowers in this area are yellow pealed. How many other varieties do you see? The coneflower is common on upland prairie. It is a shallow rooted perennial orb native to the plains.
Marker 19 Here you are entering the second draw of the three you will cross. You will note the shelf fungi are very common. Apparently, a number of dead, standing trees have been that way for a number of years, giving the mycelia and thread-like roots of the fungi a chance to spread and grow and reveal themselves to you in their work of living and recycling the stores of energy in the dead standing trees. There are a number of holes in trees, evidence of woodpeckers and other birds making good use of the dead standing trees by nesting and resting their young. The homes are only temporary. During the construction of this trail, two dead trees suddenly fell on their own in the presence of workers, suggesting a need for a bit of caution on your part!

You must cross the stream at the footbridge. This swiftly moving perennial stream originates from a spring about half a mile up the draw on private land. There were at least four military expeditions which camped near the spring that feeds this stream, half a mile south of this spot: the Sully expedition of 1864 to the Battle of Killdeer Mountains, the Stanley survey of the Great Northern Railroad of 1873, the Custer expedition of 1874 to the Black Hills, and the fateful Custer journey to the Little big Horn in 1876.

If you sit still here, the things that you will see in the trees and in the water are surprising. The water contains many interesting insects. Their life cycle depends on the plant debris and microorganisms which are washed into the stream.

The tall plants you see on your left as you cross the bridge are stinging nettle. They can make you itch. The fine hairs on the shaft of the plant inject alkaloids into your skin and clothes which can be very irritable to sensitive skin.
Marker 20 This marker is directly west of the perennial stream. You are on a divide between the stream and a small dry tributary. Ahead of you and to the northwest, there is and artificial nest box for a screech owl. One of the limiting factors for wildlife in the area is good nesting habitat. Some wildlife enthusiasts are cooperating with BLM to provide good habitat for those species, which are missing an element of their ecological niche, like a nesting hole.
Marker 21 You have just completed the second crossing of the stream. Note the tall reed canarygrass to your left. It not only tolerates, but need a lot of moisture.
Marker 22 Many bushes should be part of the components of this woody draw. Without grazing, they should begin to repopulate the draw.
Marker 23 To the north you can see an old eroded road cut leading into the draw. You can see the old road better on top at marker 25. The first settlers in the vicinity, W.A. and Sherman Slater ran a stage stop on the old Bismarck to Fort Keogh Stage Line within half a mile of this spot. There may be features like this one on the property which date back to one hundred and twenty years ago from the old Bismarck to Fort Keogh Stage Line, one of the Custer expeditions, or the Sully expedition to the Killdeer Mountains.
Marker 24 You are in the prairie again. To the south, notice the boundary between two microenvironments as revealed by some of the larger buffaloberry bushes. The native buffaloberry is a deciduous, large shrub of small tree with the branches being somewhat spine tipped. The fruits are eaten by birds and small animals. American Indians and pioneers preserved the fruits by drying. These bushes are intruding into some of the moister forest microenvironments, thus growing larger; yet it isn’t moist enough for the forest species to really take advantage of the site. During several wet years, some forest species may be able to take advantage of the marginal forest habitat at the upper parts of the draw, become established, and out-compete prairie species for years to come.

The odd looking flat spot to the west is commonly called a "panspot". The panspot is formed by the removal of the topsoil or "A" horizon exposing the next layer of soil which is commonly known as the subsoil or "B" horizon. The subsoil has a heavy accumulation of clay, giving it a high water-holding capacity. If that is the case, the plants should be doing wonderfully well here, shouldn’t they? They do look healthy enough…for cactus! You may ask what cactus is doing here in an area that should be supporting reed canary grass of other moisture loving species. It turns out that the subsoil has a heavy salt and sodium content. Salt increases the osmotic pressure in the soil, which means that it takes much more effort for the plant to draw water from the soil. The practical effect is that in this soil, a plant will be able to get less water even though the ground is wet. So the only plants that can survive are xeric plants, or plants adapted to dry climate.

The white sage-like plant nearby is called winterfat, also known as white sage of sweetage. This is excellent forage relished by all livestock, deer, and elk. That is why there isn’t much of it on the property. It is high in protein content. Winter ranges, with an abundance of this plant, are said to make livestock "winterfat", hence the origin of its name!
Marker 25 Note the grasses colonizing the former access road, which was nearly bare in 1993.

Green needlegrass is an abundant part of the native prairie complex here. It is an especially palatable grass, but since the seed heads aren’t needle-sharp, it does not cause livestock problems like needle and thread grass or porcupine grass sometimes do.

Forty feet to the west of this marker you can find a patch of daisy fleabane. Look for a many branched plant with small, daisy-like, white petalled, yellow centered flowers. The story of how this plant got its name must be very interesting. The name suggest that it may have once been used in some way to keep fleas away before we had such nice aids to hygiene such as soap, hot water, and houses built without an eastern floor!

Fifty feet to the west of this marker you can find a patch of switchgrass, a small wispy grass much more common to wet lowlands areas. Switchgrass is consumed by livestock as long as the stems remain green.

If you are lucky, you may notice ground nesting birds during your hike. If you do, please don’t touch the nest, eggs, or young nestling. Just observe briefly, watch for the mother bird, who may try to draw you away, and move on.

Marker 26 This bench or plateau shows where the stream used to be before another cycle of stream downcutting or erosion brought the stream down to its current level. This increase in erosiveness might have been caused by a shift in climate.
Marker 27 There is more reed canarygrass fifteen feet to your right. Thirty feet to your right is some prairie cordgrass, one of the tallest grasses that is native to North America. Prairie cordgrass occupies wet soils and is somewhat alkali tolerant. Leaves and stems of prairie cordgrass were used by pioneers for thatching roofs and covering haystacks. American Indians thatched lodges with cordgrass before covering the grass mat with soil.
Marker 28 If you walk to the east you can actually "walk on water" in a sense. You can walk out into the tall grass on a mat of thick dead vegetation almost all the way across the drainage until you come to the swiftly moving part of the stream. If you stand there awhile, and especially if you wiggle your feet a little, you will sink in the vegetation, and will find that you are standing in a slow moving part of the stream which flows beneath you.
Marker 29 Six feet to the northeast and twelve feet to the northwest you can find small clumps of purple prairie clover in the first half of the summer. It has many tiny purple flowers on pods or cones sticking up at the top of the plant.

Occasionally, while hiking on the property, you will see faint trails in use. Usually, these will be evidence of deer with their larger heavier bodies, but other animals such as porcupines, skunks, coyotes, etc., may use some of these trails also.
Marker 30 Silverleaf scurfpea is common here. It is the plant that resembles alfalfa, but with bigger leaves that are green on the top and silvery on the bottom. It has tiny intensely dark blue flowers in the mid-summer. It the fall, the plants will dry up and break off at ground level, allowing the plant to break away, tumble with the wind, and scatter seed.

Wild flowers are different on the prairie every month. You can come back to the trail several times in a summer and usually see different species. You will, however, see the largest numbers and greatest variety when soil is moist.

On the way to the next marker you will pass a large bush. It is a common woody plant on the prairie, the hawthorn. It will have either white or pink blossoms and small red fruits or "apples" and some rather fearsome looking thorns.
Marker 31 The grass populating much of the slope behind you is called little bluestem. New shoots look somewhat bluish-green when young and growing, hence the name, but they turn reddish-brown as they age. It maintains a bunch appearance with openness of cover between plants.
Marker 32 As you observe the woody draw ahead you will see that the brushy component of the ecosystem, which should be a part of it, doesn’t exist. A rest from grazing will be needed to allow seeds long dormant in the ground, or brought in by the movement of birds and animals like deer, to sprout and fill in their "niche" or naturally evolved place in the ecosystem.
Marker 33 Above you is a broken off tree. This is often the start of an opportunity for water to accumulate in the exposed core of the tree, rot it, and thus provide a hollow for squirrels, birds, wasps, bees, and other creations.
Marker 34 You will find prairie junegrass, a native grass species about three feet west of the marker. It resembles a spear in shape because of the way the seeds spread out from the stem.

You can also find groundplum milkvetch, or buffalo bean, about twenty feet west of the marker on the edge of an old cattle trail. It will have purple to violet flowers which blossom in May or June. The fruits, resembling a small plum, are hollow, and colored purple and green.

Note the badly eroded trail, used in the past by both vehicles and livestock. The trail is slowly healing. The shallowest cuts, higher on the slopes, heal up first by plants becoming established and holding onto soil. These plants, and the soil which they hold onto, will both soak up falling rainwater and melting snow, and will retard the flow of water, making it easier for the plants farther down the slope in deeper gullies to become established.
Marker 35 In 1993, the area to the west was a six foot high headcut caused, to a great extent, by the trails concentrating water and draining it directly into the draw in one spot. The height of the dropoff and the lack of vegetation on the floor of the woody draw allowed the water to expend all of its energy at the base of the dropoff, scouring or undercutting the bank, and causing the headcut to advance up the draw. As the shallower trail, which you saw at the last marker, heals over with vegetation, it will slow water flow and lessen the amount of it passing through the draw. A little soil will still slump down from the headcut, softening the slope. Vegetation will finally become established here, completing the process of erosion control. Soil will still erode faster than the rate of new soil formation.
Marker 36 Fifty feet to the right and fifty feet behind you, notice the hollow trees. Could these be the home to a hive? A hive needs a hole big enough for a bee to get through, one at a time.

Here the bottom of the drainage is being assisted in recovery by natural dams caused by deadfall trees with vegetation being caught in them and holding back moisture and soil. This draw has lost more soil than many of the others. By looking at the base of the trees, you can see that a foot of soil or more has been eroded from around them. This draw seems to be recovering more slowly in terms of vegetation. Apparently, more of the native vegetation was eliminated from this drainage than from some of the others.
Marker 37 Walk carefully and watch your step. Many branches are hidden on the forest floor under grass. These branches and rotting trees provide food or energy for soil building insects, worms, and other creatures. The dead wood ends up being reduced to humus. Humus is the well-decomposed, more or less stable part of the organic matter of the soil. It is useful for plant growth because it helps hold moisture and minerals used by the plants.
   
Marker 39 The plant resembling sage around the marker is cudweed sagewort. Some American Indians used cudweed sagewort for medicinal purposes and religious ceremonies.
Marker 40 The draw going to the west is normally dry, an intermittent stream. But here where it joins the main perennial stream, it borrows water from it, and so has about 100 feet of riparian vegetation and marsh.
Marker 41 You are in the middle of a crested wheatgrass monoculture. It is very lush, as are the smooth bromegrass monocultures you have gone through. A monoculture is much less valuable to wildlife, since it tends to favor just a small number of species which can use it to survive, over the great numbers in the ecosystem which are also trying to survive.
Marker 42 Our long term plan on the Schnell Ranch Recreation Area is to replant to native species the one third of the property which is in introduced species. Introduced species can and are useful for production agriculture, but in an ecosystem for large numbers of species, they add complications because sometimes there are unanticipated effects. Sometimes the introduced species can get out of control, like noxious weeds, such as leafy spurge and spotted knapweed in the upper midwest, rabbits in Australia, zebra mussels in the great lakes stated, and kudzu in the deep south. They may not have any enemies in the new environment growing exorbitantly, thus taking away living space from other creatures and causing extinction. We will never be able to eliminate the introduced species, but hope to reduce the numbers to be just another minor, but new component of the ecosystem.
Marker 43 Back at the main pond area, you see the greater variety of vegetation provided as a result of the confluence of prairie lands, woodlands, still pond water, swamp or march, and the last of the flowing stream before the pond. The animal life is similarly varied here.

Ducks tend to use the main portions of the pond while mating and sometimes for nesting. The nestlings tend to use the shallow backwaters of the pond because of the abundance of insect life, so it’s an important a source of protein for the swiftly growing ducklings.

This spot gives a nice overlook of the whole pond. In the spring you can see baby ducklings, and a variety of bird life. Birds frequenting the pond include the following species: mallard, gadwall, blue winged teal, pintail, green winged teal, shoveler, widgeon, ringneck, lesser scaup, wood duck, hooded merganser, bufflehead, redhead, eastern bluebird, mountain bluebird, robin, tree swallow, barn swallow, rough wing swallow, vesper sparrow, white crown sparrow, grasshopper sparrow, chipping sparrow, lard sparrow, harris sparrow, fox sparrow, song sparrow, tree sparrow, lark bunting, bobolink, white breasted nuthatch, hairy woodpecker, downy woodpecker, northern flicker, northern oriole, blackheaded grosbeak, yellow throat, yellow warbler, yellow-rumped warbler, myrtle warbler, western kingbird, eastern kingbird, northern harrier, red wing blackbird, house wren, sora rail, wilson’s snipe, and many more.
Marker 44 The pond is divided by a former access road, which actually is helpful to observers by making it easier to see wildlife, and to get to different areas of the pond. Unfortunately this pond has become much shallower through the years with the effects of sediment filing it in. If this had not happened, there could have been a much deeper part of the pond, which could support fish along with the marsh and swamp species, which would have been at the upper end. The sedimentation is partially a natural effect, but has at times been greatly accelerated by agricultural practices. We hope that management as a primitive recreation area will end these negative effects and show the potential of the land for supporting native flora and fauna in southwestern North Dakota.
