Lepidoptera

By Alexandra Dressler

utterflies have an exoskeleton.  Their bodies are divided into three main parts-head, thorax, and abdomen.  They have three pairs of jointed legs on the thorax.  Butterflies have a pair of large, oval, compound eyes.  Its’ mouth is located between its’ eyes.  When it’s not feeding, the butterfly keeps its’ tongue, or proboscis, curled up below its’ face.  Two antennas are on the butterflies head.  The thorax is the middle part of the body.  It contains the powerful flight muscles that bear the legs and wings.  The wings are made of two membranes with a network of stiff veins between the layers.  There are two fore wings and two hind wings.  The abdomen contains the heart, respiratory, digestive systems, and reproductive organs.

utterflies lay eggs.  The egg hatches into a larva called a caterpillar.  When a caterpillar reaches its’ full size, it prepares to complete its metamorphosis, which takes place inside the pupa.  Hanging from a twig, the caterpillar shed its’ skin to reveal the pupa underneath.  The pupal stage may last anywhere from one week to several years.  When its’ development is complete, the adult butterfly splits the pupal shell and crawls out.  Most adult butterflies live for about one or two weeks.

he butterfly can be found almost anywhere in the world except Antarctica and the oceans.  In the tropics you will find the widest diversity of butterflies.  Butterflies like open, sunny settings.  Butterflies may be found in flowery fields, meadows, and hillsides; along stream banks and the edges of woods; and in clearings, glades, and other natural reserves.  Large numbers of butterflies can be found in arctic and alpine tundra, and deserts following spring or summer rains.  They’re even found in urban settings.

dult butterflies mostly eat flower nectar and other sugars, like the sap of wounded trees, rotting fruit, and fluids excreted by certain vines.  Some male butterflies suck up dissolved salts from mud puddles.  They’ll also eat vertebrate feces and dead animals to get nutrients like carbon, nitrogen, and amino acids.

he butterfly probably arose between 200 million and 300 million years ago from insects similar to present-day caddisflies.  The earliest known fossils of primitive moths, found in Lebanese amber and Siberian sediments, are 100 million to 130 million years old.  Many butterfly fossils are found in shale about 38 million years old from the Forissant Fossil Beds of Colorado.  Some of theses fossil butterflies closely resemble present-day species.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do bug zappers really work?

Studies have shown that bug zappers do not accomplish the purpose for which they were meant.  Most insects caught are nonbiting and are nonharmful.  Predators and parasites who naturally help to control the pest population are also caught.  Bug zappers are useless and can actually produce the opposite purpose for which they were meant.

How do you get rid of

beetles in your flour?

Beetles in your house originate from infested grain or from dry, stored food products.  To control these pests infested items should be thrown away or salvaged by freezing for one week or heating in a 140 degree oven for fifteen minutes. You should empty and thoroughly vacuum clean cupboards or shelves holding infested items.

3 Interesting Insects

Mining Bees

Mining bees are also known as andrenid bees.  They are found on the lawn, especially dry areas with little vegetation.  The bees nest in the ground in cylindrical tunnels dug by the females.  Entrance to the tunnels is marked with small piles of soil.  The bees are docile and not likely to sting unless handled or threatened.

Giant Water Bugs

The Giant Water Bug can be found in lakes, ponds and quiet streams.  They are good fliers.  They’re attracted to light so you might see them under a street lamp or in the yard.  They are harmless, but if you handle them carelessly, you may get a painful bite.                                                 

Horsehair Worm

 Horsehair worms get their names because they resemble the “hair of a horse’s tail or mane.”  They are no bigger around than a kite string, and they’re very long.  The entire horsehair worm grew and developed as a parasite inside the body cavity of crickets and other large insects such as grasshoppers, katydids, beetles and cockroaches.  When the parasitized crickets are thirsty and go to water to drink the horsehair worm emerges from the insect’s body and swims away in the water.  Horsehair worms are harmless.

INSECT RECIPES

Bug Blox

Ingredients:

2 large packages gelatin
2 1/2 cups boiling water (do not add cold water)

Directions:

Stir boiling water into gelatin. Dissolve completely. Stir in dry-roasted leafhoppers. Pour mixture slowly into 13 x 9 inch pan. Chill at least 3 hours. BLOX will be firm after 1 hour, but may be difficult to remove from pan. Cutting blox: dip bottom pan in warm water 15 seconds to loosen gelatin. Cut shapes with cookie cutters all the way through gelatin. Lift with index finger or metal spatula. If blox stick, dip pan again for a few seconds.

Crackers and Cheese Dip with Candied Crickets

Ingredients:

8 oz. cream cheese
4 oz. shredded cheddar cheese
1 tsp. Worchestershire sauce
2 tsp. chopped onions
1 tsp. chopped green pepper
2 tsp Miracle Whip®
candied crickets

Directions:

Soften cream cheese. Introduce remaining ingredients.

Spread mixture on cracker and top with a candied cricket.

The Fluttering Butterball

                                            (Volito Adulatio Teres)

        he Fluttering Butterball goes through complete metamorphosis.  It has an egg, larva, and pupa stage, in that order, before it becomes an adult. 

                        Egg       Pupa     Larva

 

he Fluttering Butterball has four antennas.  It has two compound eyes.  The mouth is just like a human’s.  It has four triangular shaped wings that have bright stripes.  The thorax has brown wooly hair.   Six legs come off of the thorax.  The feet have a thin layer of ice on the bottom.  Its’ abdomen is striped yellow.   

  Latin name for the  Fluttering Butterball is Volito Adulatio Teres, and it is a member of the order Lepidoptera.  It lives in the cold snowy land of Antarctica.  To keep from freezing, the Fluttering Butterball has developed a brown wooly layer on its’ thorax.  It has a thin layer of hair on the top of its’ head to keep heat from escaping.  Its’ triangular shaped wings enable the Fluttering Butterball to fly really fast.  The Fluttering Butterball goes through complete metamorphosis.  It has an egg, larva, and pupa stage before it becomes an adult.

 luttering Butterballs eat snow bugs that can be easily caught even though they are fast because Fluttering Butterballs are faster yet.  Snow bugs are attracted to Fluttering Butterballs because they have such brightly colored wings.  Polar bears are very fond of the Fluttering Butterball’s crunchy good taste, so Fluttering Butterballs try to stay away from them.  The four antennas on its’ head enable it to detect polar bears from far away.  The Fluttering Butterball has a thin layer of ice on the bottom of its’ feet so that it can still get around and escape predators when it’s tired of flying.  It just slides along on the ice.  This is extremely important because the Fluttering Butterball has no way of defending itself except to escape its’ predator.
