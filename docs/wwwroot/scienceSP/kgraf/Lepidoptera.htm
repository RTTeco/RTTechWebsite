Lepidoptera

By: Kasandra Graf

          Butterflies have scale less, threadlike antennae with a club on the end. They have mainly brightly colored wings. The wing color and pattern play a key role in mate recognition and courtship. They fly mainly during daylight. Some tropical species fly at dawn or dusk, and few are nocturnal. The largest butterflies have a wingspan up to 25 cm. The smallest butterflies may barely exceed 1 cm.    

           Moths have an antennae in various forms but often look feathery. Many are also brightly colored, but most are dull shades of brown. Females attract males by giving off a chemical which is released form Pheromone glands. Most moths fly at night. Many fly during the day, especially in colder climates where the evening temperatures often drop to freezing. Moths are attracted by light. They tend to travel in the direction of light.  The largest species are the giant silkworm moths of Asia. They have a wingspan as long as 30 cm., and as small as a few mm.

Banana Worm Bread

Ingredients:

  • 1/2 cup shortening
  • 3/4 cup sugar
  • 2 bananas, mashed
  • 2 cups flour
  • 1 teaspoon soda
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup chopped nuts
  • 2 eggs
  • 1/4 cup dry-roasted army worms

Directions:

Mix together all ingredients. Bake in greased loaf pan at 350 degrees for about 1 hour.

Chocolate Chirpie Chip Cookies

Ingredients:

  • 2 1/4 cup flour
  • 1 tsp. baking soda
  • 1 tsp. salt
  • 1 cup butter, softened
  • 3/4 cup sugar
  • 3/4 cup brown sugar
  • 1 tsp. vanilla
  • 2 eggs
  • 1 12-ounce chocolate chips
  • 1 cup chopped nuts
  • 1/2 cup dry-roasted crickets

Directions:

Preheat oven to 375. In small bowl, combine flour, baking soda and salt; set aside. In large bowl, combine butter, sugar, brown sugar and vanilla; beat until creamy. Beat in eggs. Gradually add flour mixture and insects, mix well. Stir in chocolate chips. Drop by rounded measuring teaspoonfuls onto ungreased cookie sheet. Bake for 8-10 minutes.

    Butterflies and moths undergo complete metamorphosis. Their life cycle consists of four stages: egg, larva (caterpillar), pupa (cocoon or chrysalis), and adult. Adult females usually lay eggs on a plant, which acts as a food source. They have elastic cylindrical bodies, simple eyes, chewing mouthparts, three pairs of legs on the thorax, and usually five pairs of prolegs on the abdomen. Prolegs are the legs that only larvae have. They continuously eat, periodically shedding their skin as they grow to hundreds of times their original size. Finally they reach the stage where they spin cocoons and become pupae. During pupation, the structures of the larvae are totally transformed; internal systems are reorganized and adult external structures develop.

    Adult butterflies and moths feed on wide variety of substances. They feed on nectar, pollen, rotting fruit, carrion, dung, urine, and other plant and animal secretions. However they mainly seek flower nectar. They help to pollinate plants by carrying pollen form flower to flower. In many species, such as sulfur butterflies, egg production is impossible without nectar meals. In others, such as the checkerspot butterflies, unfed females lay about half as many eggs as those provided nectar. A few adult moths do not have functional mouthparts and lay all their eggs without obtaining nourishment.

    Larvae of butterflies and moths are usually found feeding on a single species or a few related species of plants. This is a result in the isolation of many species in colonies in particular habitats. Other species have a more extensive range, especially those that lay eggs on widely distributed or weedy plants. Many migrate thousands of kilometers to spend the winter in large aggregations. Competition between larvae for host plants can produce a different population cycle. Some changes in population size are due primarily to climatic conditions.

    Butterflies and moths are found in a wide variety of habitats. They live in the tundra to the rain forest and from below sea level to nearly 6000 meters in elevation. In tropical areas many fly throughout the year. Continual good weather and and ample amount of resources allow for rapid larval development and long adult life. In temperate habitats butterflies and moths enter and inactive stage, or diapause, during their development, to avoid severe weather conditions. Diapause may occur in the egg, larval, pupal, or adult stage. In snowy climates, winter hibernation is common. In areas with hot, dry seasons, summer diapause (estivation) is in rule. Larval development occurs mainly in warmer climates.

 

    The Magestique butterfly comes from the insect order Lepidoptera. It's habitat consists of lots of sun, moisture, and plants. It is found only in Tropical regions. It has siphoning tubes to get nectar from flowers and other plants. It's colors are used to warn off predators, and for mating.
