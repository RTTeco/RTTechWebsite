Hymenoptera

    Hymenoptera is an order of insects containing more than 100,000 species.  Some characteristics that all Hymenopterans have in common are two pairs of membranous wings, although some may be wingless, forewings are larger than hind wings and are held together by small hooks. Females usually have a hardened ovipositor, which may be modified for sawing, piercing or stinging. Most hymenopterans have a constriction between the first 2 segments of the abdomen, which is known as a 'wasp waist'. The Hymenoptera is divided into 2 suborders. The Symphyta (sawflies) who have no discernible waist and the Apocrita (ants, bees and wasps) who have a distinct waist. Chewing (mandibulate) mouthparts, although in some species such as bees the lower lip is modified to form a tongue and  compound eyes.  More information available at www.hymenoptera.com

Frequently asked questions and their answers.
Do bug zappers really work? No, they kill far more helpful or harmless bugs than mosquitoes.
Why are beetles in flour and how do you get rid of them? Beetles lay eggs in the flour and lie dormant until proper conditions arise.  To get rid of them you can either freeze the product or burn it.

 

Insect Recipes

Chocolate Chirpie Chip Cookies

Ingredients:

2 1/4 cup flour
1 tsp. baking soda
1 tsp. salt
1 cup butter, softened
3/4 cup sugar
3/4 cup brown sugar
1 tsp. vanilla
2 eggs
1 12-ounce chocolate chips
1 cup chopped nuts
1/2 cup dry-roasted crickets

Directions:

Preheat oven to 375. In small bowl, combine flour, baking soda and salt; set aside. In large bowl, combine butter, sugar, brown sugar and vanilla; beat until creamy. Beat in eggs. Gradually add flour mixture and insects, mix well. Stir in chocolate chips. Drop by rounded measuring teaspoonfuls onto ungreased cookie sheet. Bake for 8-10 minutes. www.chirpiechipcookies.com

The Spotted Buffalo Wasp

     The spotted buffalo wasp is native to the Midwest region.  It belongs to the order Hymenoptera. The Latin name for the Buffalo Wasp is bovis maculosus caristrum.  It has a lifespan of 4 weeks in which it matures into an adult and reproduces.  The females of the species have stingers that secrete a very lethal toxin.  One sting from one of these can easily kill a human, livestock, or a buffalo from which it gets its name.  In the 1800s the wasp killed thousands of buffalo that would come to close to the nesting site.  The female wasps secrete a hormone that attracts the males.  After mating the female wasps will usually kill the male.  The female wasp then stings a tree.  The tree then swell slightly where it was stung.  The female then lays her eggs and dies almost immediately after.  The wasps then lie dormant in the eggs until the temperature reaches 80 degrees Fahrenheit.  They then mature into adults while in the egg.  This process takes about a week so the wasps have 3 weeks in which to find a mate and reproduce.  Spotted Buffalo Wasps are very rare so it is difficult for them to find mates.
