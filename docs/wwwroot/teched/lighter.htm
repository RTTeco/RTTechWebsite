EXPLORING TECHNOLOGY
AIR TRANSPORTATION

Probably the most inventive and imaginative transportation ideas have come in the field of flight. For thousands of years, people wondered why birds could fly. Many attempts were made by the Greeks and Chinese to discover ways to make people fly. The Greek legend of Icarus describes how wings of feathers were strapped to his arms, and how he crashed to his death. Even great inventors like Leonardo da Vinci thought people should be able to fly by flapping some kind of a wing device. History is full of stories of birdmen who tried to copy birds in flight, but were never able to turn their dreams into reality. What really started air flight were dreams of going up into the sky, not a special need to transport people or things somewhere else. People didn't think about how aircraft could help them in the beginning. You can divide aircraft into lighter-than-air vehicles and heavier-than-air vehicles. Lighter-than-air vehicles float in air depending on their weight and volume. Heavier-than-air vehicles must supply power to fly. The first really successful aircraft of any kind was the hot air balloon designed by Frenchmen Joseph and Etienne Montgolfier in 1783. They did not know what really made their balloon go up. They thought maybe some unknown, mysterious gas was released from burning wood! Do you know why hot-air balloons go up? This principle of lighter-than-air flight was used in many more designs as people experimented with hydrogen-filled and hot-air-filled balloons to carry people up. Although the first flights were unmanned, they pointed the way to later flights with ducks, chickens and sheep as passengers. Eventually on September 21, 1783 Pilatre de Rozier and Marquis D'Arlandes became the first men to make an aerial journey, ascending in a montgolfier-type balloon and flying over Paris for 25 minutes at an altitude of 3,000 feet. These hot-air balloons are one form of transportation. However, they are also used for recreational purposes. The use of hot-air balloons has increased in the past few years. In fact, many cities now have hot-air balloon contests for accuracy and distance of flight. The technology is now affordable and can be learned easily. The balloon flies because it is filled with hot air which is less dense than the air around it. When the air in the balloon cools, it gets heavier and sinks. Hot air is put into the balloon from the exhaust of large propane burners. Technologically, a hydrocarbon fuel is converted by combustion into thermal energy. Once the balloon is aloft, the thermal energy inside is lost to the outside. As this happens, the balloon slowly descends. Control of the position and height of the balloon is determined by turning on the propane burners for a certain length of time. Usually, someone tracks the balloon over the land to act as support when is lands.

HOT AIR BALLOONS

You and your partner are going to make a hot air balloon during this part of your rotation. The materials you will need are:   

12 sheets of tissue paper (approx. 20" x 30")
White glue
Scissors
Tape
Straight pins (optional)
The pattern
Wire
Construction paper
Clothes pins

Lets get started.

Step 1. You and your partner will be constructing one balloon as a team or you may each construct one for yourself but you do need to work together. Select 12 pieces of tissue paper from the supply in the classroom. You might want to consider some sort of color scheme or arrangement of colors. The tissue paper measures about 20 inches by 30 inches. When finished, make sure you leave the drawer in neat order.

Step 2. You are going to glue six pairs of two sheets together to form six large panels approximately 20 inches by 60 inches. To have enough room to glue the panels together roll out the tissue sheets and work in the manufacturing laboratory.

Step 3. Lay out two pieces of tissue on the work bench. Using white glue, put a continuous small bead of glue about 1/4 inch from the edge of the 20 inch side. Overlap the edged of the sheets and pat the glue area down. Remember you are trying to make six large panels 20" by 60". Your fingers will get sticky, so you may need a damp paper towel to keep your hands clean. Lay the two sheets you have glued aside, and continue gluing pairs together until you have six large panels. You need to be careful that you do not get so much glue on the seam that it soaks through and sticks to the other panels. You need to fold the panels carefully, and store them until the next class period. See the instructor to place your sheets in storage.

Step 4. Clean up the glue from the work table and put any other equipment in its proper place.

Step 5. When the six panels are dry, stack them on top of each other, and then lay the pattern on top. The pattern is located in the same drawer as the tissue paper. You will want to use approximately 8 straight pins to hold the pattern and the stack of tissue paper together (optional). If the pattern is longer or wider than your stack of tissues do not worry.

Step 6. Carefully cut around the pattern. If the pattern is larger than your tissue continue to cut but adjust your balloon according to the size of your paper by following the same radii and tapers.

Step 7. When you have cut out the entire shape, remove the straight pins. Fold the pattern carefully and return it to the drawer. Keep a few sheets of tissue paper for the top and for patching. Throw the rest in the trash.

Step 8. You are now ready to begin gluing the panels of your balloon together. You will take 2 panels off your stack, place one on top of the other, so the bottom panel edge extends 1/2 inch past the top panel.

Step 9. Fold the bottom panel edge up over the top, then unfold. Put a continuous bead of glue 1/4 inch from the edge of the top panel and pat the tissue paper into the glue.

Step 10. Take panel 3 and place it on top of panel 2 so the opposite edge of panel 2 is extended 1/2 inch. Again put a line of glue on the edge and fold over the edge of panel 2 and pat in onto the glue. Be careful not to use an excess amount of glue or the panel might stick together where they should not. Continue until all six panels have been placed on top and folded and glued. If you get caught with your balloon half glued together at the end of the class period carefully fold it and place it in storage.

Step 11. Continue to place panels on top until all panels have been glued. If you have glued all but the last seam of the balloon, it is time to flip the whole stack over.

Step 12. Fold the inside glued edges back inside out of the way.

Step 13. Extend the bottom edge of panel 2 and 6 out 1/2 inch and put a thin line of glue on the top edge about 1/4 inch in from the edge. Fold over the bottom and pat it into the glued line. You should no have all edges of the six panels glued together.

Step 14. Cut a piece of tissue for the top. It can be any shape. A hexagon or octagon, or a circle. Depending on how your balloon went together. Trace out and cut out a piece of tissue from the few that you saved. This will be glued to the top of your balloon. Place a coffee can inside your balloon and squeeze the balloon down around the can so the top of your balloon is over the can. Apply a bead of glue and press the top of your balloon in place

.Step 15. Obtain a piece of wire it is found in the same drawer as the rest of the material.

Step 16. Form a hoop with the wire and tape the two ends together with black electrical tape. Be careful so the wire does not spring free. This hoop will fit into the bottom of your balloon. Place the hoop inside your balloon, apply a bead of glue round and fold the bottom of your balloon over the hoop and pat the tissue in place to permanently trap the hoop. Glue and work about three inches at a time as you glue and pat. Clothes pins help in holding the wire in place.

Step 17. Obtain a piece of construction paper. Using the paper cutter, cut two strips the long way about 2 inches wide. Tape the two strips together so they will be about 28 inches long. Fold the strip in half, so the strip is in a vee shape about 28" long.

Step 18. Using a stapler, staple the vee shape strip over the bottom of your balloon and hoop. This makes neat a looking edge.

Step 19. Obtain a piece of string at least 15 feet long if you would like a tether, and with a paper punch, punch a hole through the construction paper and the balloon; tie one end of the string to the balloon.

Step 20. Inspect your balloon for any holes or tears. Use the pieces of saved material to patch your balloon. It would be a good idea to save these pieces in case you need them in the future

.Step 21. You are now ready to launch your balloon.

Launch Conditions

One thing to remember is that the greater the temperature difference between the outside air and the heated air inside the balloon the higher your balloon will go. A cool crisp morning is the optimum for hot air balloon launches. The air must be calm for the launch. The launch site should be level, well away from buildings, power lines, and water.

Launching the Balloon

Step 1. Construct your hot air balloon as described in the module written material.

Step 2. Review these Safety Considerations.

Make sure you notify the instructor before launching your balloon.

While most of the launcher will be cool to the touch, students should be cautioned that the grill near the top of the launcher chimney is very hot while the launcher is lit.

Student should never lean over the launcher while it is lit.

A fire extinguisher should be on site when operating the launcher.

This unit uses propane cylinders as a fuel source. Store propane  canisters away from sources of heat and sparks.

Balloon fabric is flammable. Avoid overheating by keeping the fabric away  from the hot air exhaust. The launcher should be operated outdoors only.

It is advisable that all students, except those involved in launching, keep a safe distance of at least 10 feet from the launcher.

Step 3. Have igniter ready as you open the flow valve slightly. Insert the lighter into the hole in the side of the chimney of the launcher, and squeeze the trigger to ignite the gas within the launcher. Open the valve fully.

Step 4. Have two students securely grasp the bottom of the balloon with their finger tips. The top of the balloon should be held up by a third student if needed.

Step 5. The students then quickly position the opening of the balloon over the top of the balloon launcher. The top of the balloon should be held as high as possible over the launcher until the balloon is sufficiently full of hot air to keep its shape.

Step 6. Students should keep a good grasp on the balloon until it is completely full of hot air.

Step 7. Once the pull from the buoyant hot air is steady and strong, release the balloon into the air.

Step 8. When finished, shut the flow valve to turn off the launcher. Step 9. Unscrew the propane canister and store the launcher and the propane safely.

Step 10. Fill out your launch report.

TISSUE BALLOON PATTERN (For instructors)

To make pattern for the balloon get a piece of paper 2 feet x 5 feet long

Butcher paper works, two sheets of tag board also works.

Fold the paper lengthwise in half.

On the folded edge, measure off and mark every 6 inches from the bottom to the top. At the six inch locations, measure the distance from the edge and place a mark according to the following schedule.  

0"           3 1/2"   

6"           3 1/2"

12"       5"

18"       6 3/8"

24"      8"

30"      9 3/8"

36       9 5/8"

42"      9"

48"     6 1/2"

54"     3 5/8"

60"      1 1/2"     

Using a pencil connect the marks with a uniform curved line.

Cut out the pattern like a paper doll. You Can also cut  a circle, hexagon or a octagon shape about 5" to 8" across the flats. This will fill the hole at the top.

You can actually take great liberty with the pattern development. You can create balloon shapes of your own.
