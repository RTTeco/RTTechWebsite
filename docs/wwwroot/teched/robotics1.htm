EXPLORING TECHNOLOGY
ROBOTICS AND ROBOTIC PROGRAMMING

Movies and television cartoons often portray robots. Many times these media images do not match with reality. Just a few years ago, a robot invasion seemed ready to displace humans in factories, take over office tasks, and maybe even run the business. Of course the invasion hasn't occurred. In retrospect, the projected image of robots surpassing humans in all characteristics was just that, and imagination. In real life most robots are industrial robots. The roots of their development lie in the effort to automate some or all of the operations required on the factory floor. Many businesses today employ robots. Robots work at a variety of tasks, Some robots sort parts for manufacturing. A great number of robots do welding. Robots are used in underwater and space exploration.
In the United States most industrial robots are employed in automobile plants. They work alongside people building cars. They are involved in a number of processes. Robots are used for palletizing where they lift and stack materials for shipment. Robots handle hot metals in the forging and foundry processes. They weld in difficult positions without resting. Since they never tire, their work is accurate. Since they never fool around or waste effort, they are efficient workers too. Robots in industry are truly a benefit. They assist people in factories to make or produce more goods, cheaper and easier. Economists call this "increased productivity". While robots can do many useful jobs, we must remember that all robots are machines. Machines are designed by people to serve human needs. As machines, all robots are controlled by people. Machines differ for people in important ways. They do not think, dream, image, sleep, eat, feel emotions or have desires. Very few robots resemble human beings physically either. Some are very big and other are very small. They rarely have faces or complete bodies, because they don't need all the human body parts for their jobs. As humans go, robots are a disappointment. But as production machines, robots are increasingly proving themselves as cost effective tools. Robotics engineers design various robots to do classes of tasks efficiently. So, a robot designed for working as a fixed station would not have wheels or legs for moving itself around.
Robots consist of four basic components. The manipulator, the controller, the power supply, and the end of arm tooling. The manipulator is the "arm" of the robot.
Designed much like the human arm, it takes its name. Robots have a "wrist" attached to the end of the arm. The wrist provides the angular orientation of the work piece as the arm positions the wrist in space.
The controller performs the complex computations for control of the robots joints to the proper positions and velocity so that the end of the arm follows the assigned path at the assigned speed. The controller also monitors the feedback signals from the robot's joints and actuators. True robots did not become possible until the invention of the computer in the 1940's and the progressive miniaturization of computer parts to build these complex controllers.
The power supply may be hydraulic, electric, or pneumatic depending upon the particular robot. Today most robots being introduced have electric drives.
The end of arm tooling is attached to the robot wrist and consists of a device that is application specific. This can be a gripper to close around parts, a tool holder for a welding head, an automatic screwdriver and so on. Additionally, most robot installations require other devices to position the work, deliver the parts to a particular spot for the robot to pick up, monitor safety, or secure the work piece during processing, this is call peripheral tooling.
The following is the definition of the term "robot" used by the Robot Institution of America.
A reprogramable multi-functional manipulator designed to move material, parts, tools, or specialized devices, through variable programmed motions for the performance of a variety of tasks.
Since this is a complex definition, lets break it into parts:

1. Reprogramable means something that can be given a set of computer instructions more
that once. After programming it can be erased and given a different set of instructions.

2. Multi-functional means something that can do more than one kind of work.

3. Manipulator means someone or something that handles another thing or works in by hand
or mechanically.

4. Variable refers to something that can change or be changed.

Factors for Consideration in Robot Applications

Safety - Safety is a consideration when determining whether to employ a robot or person for a given job. Some materials are unsafe for people to handle or to be exposed to, such as hazardous chemicals, radioactive material, medical waste, poisonous substances, highly flammable materials, very hot or very cold materials. Robots can be designed which are unaffected by these substances.
Certain environments where work must be done are dangerous for people or they cannot live in them at all. Deep underwater, outer space, frigid areas, very hot places, contaminated or polluted areas are not safe for people. Robots can be designed which are unaffected by these conditions.

Economics - The economics or cost of replacing a human worker with a robot is another consideration. Robots are generally very expensive to purchase, but they entail lower overhead costs than a human worker. For example, robots do not require wages, or a heated, well lit room in which to work, or vacations. Robot require maintenance, but not health insurance or holidays. People require paid salary, unlike robots. However a robot will eventually wear out, and new one must be purchased. Robots do not get tired as people do, and so they can work 24 hours a day, seven days a week, 365 days a year. Very often in industry the decision to employ a robot involves an economic decision.

Accuracy - People are not machines. Their muscles tire and they tremble when holding the same position becomes uncomfortable for them. People cannot repeat exactly the same motion as often as robots with the same degree of accuracy. In certain applications such as welding overhead or performing delicate surgery, a robot's accuracy will greatly exceed that of a person.

Capability - Robots are not people. Although they can be stronger than people, never tire, and are imperious to harsh environments or hazardous materials, there are many human capabilities they do not possess. They cannot discover people's feelings or express concern for them or make judgments about people's intentions. Some jobs require awareness of and attention to people's thoughts, feelings, and problems. Robots can not change their programming in order to do jobs which are unpredictable and require higher order thinking skills to accomplish.

The term robot itself is derived from the Czech word robota, meaning "compulsory labor". It was first used in the 1921 play R.U.R (which stands for "Rossum's Universal Robots") by the Czech novelist and playwright Karel Capek, to describe a mechanical device that looks like a human but, lacking the human sensibility, can perform only automatic, mechanical operations. The term android is now generally reserved for human like figures of this sort, ranging from robots than operate by electromechanical means in order to perform human like functions.

The early industrial robots were first used for simple "pick and place" operations, which included material handling and machine loading and unloading. As robot technology improved, they have been successfully utilized in spot welding, die casting, foundry, plastic molding, painting and finishing, and many other industrial processes. Computers today are equipped with small microprocessors that can handle the data fed them by various sensors of the surrounding environment. Making use of the principle of feedback. The commercial use of robots is spreading , with the increasing automation of factories, and they have long since become essential to many laboratory procedures. Japan is in the forefront of nations exploring robot technology. Whether the androids of science fiction will ever become reality is not yet possible to predict, because duplication of even such seemingly simple acts as bipedal walking has proved to be very difficult. Robots should continue to expand their applications; the roboarm that we have in our laboratory may be one sign of the future.

There are many different types of robots designed for use in factories, schools and homes. Robotics is a growing industry with new designs appearing constantly in the market. One method of classifying robots is by the level of technology. A low technology robot has a very limited number of program steps and no feed back. A high technology robot uses electromechanical systems to provide accurate positioning and control about the robots environment. The more axis the robot has the higher classification of the robot. The work envelope of a robot is the total three dimensional space within the gripper or end of arm tooling of the robot can be positioned. The work cell involves the area in which the robot performs tasks on or with other machines within a factory. The related machines can include a wide variety of possibilities.
