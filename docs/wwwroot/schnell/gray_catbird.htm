*THE GREY CATBIRD*

Dumetella carolinensis

  

    A slender, dark gray bird with black cap on head, long tailed with brown under tail coverts.

Voice: catlike "mewing" - Long musical and mechanical notes and phrases. Sometimes seems to mimic other birds.

Range: Breeds from British Columbia, Manitoba, and Nova Scotia south to Washington, Texas, and Georgia. Winters from the Carolinas and Gulf Coast southward.

Catbirds are mostly insect eaters and their pleasing song makes them welcome to your garden. One was spotted in our backyard foraging for insects with its tail straight up in the air. Alas, he was a very fast hopper and we were unable to get him to stand still long enough to get a picture.

Most of the gray catbird's breeding range is in north, central and eastern United States (from Oregon to New Mexico, to along the East coast), and south-central and western Canada (British Columbia, Alberta, Saskatchewan and Manitoba). The winter (migratory) range is in the extreme south-east portion of the USA, along the east coast of Mexico and the Caribbean islands.

The gray catbird is a small bird anywhere from 21-24 cm (~8.5 inches) long. Sexes are monomorphic, and both males and females are dark grey in color with a black cap and black tail feathers (National Geographic Society 1999). There is a chestnut patch underneath the tail coverts. Chicks are covered by brown or dark grey down. It has been noted that eastern populations are generally darker grey than western populations. The grey catbird has short rounded wings and long rounded tail feathers, with a short black bill, black eyes and black feet and legs (Cimprich and Moore 1995).

  Natural History

  Food Habits

The gray catbird mostly forages in treetops and on the ground. They are insectivores, and their diet may consist of hymenoptera (ants), coleoptera (beetles), diptera (flies), and lepidoptera (caterpillars and moths). They are also considered to be frugivores, as their diet may include fruits of plants such as Myrica, Sassafras, Prunus, Cordea, and Trema. However it is not known if the digestive tract is specifically modified to deal with fruit (Cimprich and Moore 1995).

Reproduction

The gray catbird builds a bulky, open nest that is low to the ground (within 2 m) (Cimprich and Moore 1995). It is made from twigs, scraps, and paper bits (Northern Michigan Birding Website 2000). Eggs are laid from April to early August (usually 2 broods). Turquoise-colored eggs are a subelliptical shape, about 17 mm by 24 mm(Cimprich and Moore 1995). Usually about 4-6 eggs are laid in one brood (Northern Michigan Birding Website 2000). Incubation is about 12-14 days and young are altricial. Parents shade the young in the nest by perching on the rim with their wings spread and breast feathers fluffed. Both males and females feed young, whose diet usually consists only of small invertebrates. Young depart from the nest ten to eleven days after hatching, and their first breeding occurs after their first complete migration, after their first year (Cimprich and Moore 1995).

  Behavior

The gray catbird is a monogamous species. Within the pair bond, the males will display during courtship by a head-down-fluffed feathers display. Pairs are territorial during the breeding season, but during migration they occur in flocks of about 10-15 birds. The males will defend a small territory about 15-18 m apart, and defend separate territories, they often change territory within the breeding season. Gray catbirds will very seldom return to the same breeding site year after year. Flight patterns of the gray catbird are short and low and they usually do not fly over wide, open spaces. Gray catbirds are preyed upon by snakes, some Falconiformes, some Strigiformes (owls), other larger passerines (American crow - Corvus brachyrhynchos), small rodents like squirrels and shrews, and the occasional domestic cat. They elicit a strong response to predators, and will display and flash wings by either attempting to attack the predator and/or flying and hopping around it, making its characteristic mew noise. (Cimprich and Moore 1995).

  Habitat

The gray catbird lives in dense thickets of woodlands, and shrub-like vegetation, and is occasionally found in residential areas (National Geographic Society 1999; Cimprich and Moore 1995). They are also found around some forest edges, not usually in areas with high conifer density. The use of forested habitat is more prevelant on the wintering ground than on the breeding ground (Cimprich and Moore 1995).

Biomes: taiga, temperate forest & rainforest

Positive

The gray catbird eats insects which are often pests to humans

(Cimprich and Moore 1995).

  Negative

Often considered a human pest because of its preference for fruit such as blueberries, and raspberries, etc. (Cimprich and Moore 1995).

Humans have increased the suitability of urban areas for catbirds by planting shrubs. However habitats have declined because of elimination of fencerows around agricultural areas, as well as human encroachment due to ever-growing human coastal populations. The gray catbird is considered a common species, but some declines in breeding populations have been noticed (Cimprich and Moore 1995).

The gray catbird has a characteristic song that consists of cat-like 'mew' noises, this is its most recognizable feature, it also make a 'quirt' or 'clucking' noise, and a 'ratchet' call (Cimprich and Moore 1995).
