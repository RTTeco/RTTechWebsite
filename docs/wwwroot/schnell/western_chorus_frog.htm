Western Chorus Frog

The Western Chorus Frog can be identified by the white or cream colored stripe that is found along its upper lip.  They are a greenish-gray to brown color and also have three dark stripes down its back.  Adults usually grow to about 1.9 to 3.8 centimeters in length.  They eat a variety of small invertebrates, including ants, flies, beetles, moths, and spiders.  Their habitat can be found in or near shallow bodies of water.  The voice of a Western Chorus Frog is  similar to the sound of a fingernail running along the teeth of a fine-toothed comb.

The breeding season of a Western Chorus Frog is from March to May.  The breeding sites include the edges of shallow ponds, ditches, wooded swamps, and flooded fields.  The female will lay 500-1500 eggs in several clusters, which usually contain 20 to 300 eggs.  The eggs will hatch in 3 to 14 days.  Western Chorus Frogs usually stay close to their breeding grounds throughout the year, and they also hibernate in these places.
