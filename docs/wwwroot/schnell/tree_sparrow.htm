Tree Swallow

(tachycineta bicolor)

Cool fact: Tree Swallows are the only swallows that make substantial use of vegetable food such as seeds and berries. They favor bayberries. Eating berries when insects aren't available helps these swallows withstand bad weather, especially during their early spring return to the breeding range.

The Tree Swallow has steel blue back with green reflections and under-parts almost a clear or pure white, a slightly forked tail and feet the color of flesh. There are no distinguishing marks between the male or female. This bird is 5 1/4 - 6" in length with a wingspan of 12-13".

Male: upperparts glossy blue-green, with long, pointed wings and notched tail; underparts pure white; bill small, black; legs pale brown

Subadult Female: upperparts dusky grey-brown, may have flecks of blue-green throughout; underparts pure white; bill and legs as in adult male

Adult Female: upperparts blue-green, usually less pure and glossy than in male; forehead often brownish; underparts pure white; bill and legs as in adult male

Juvenile: upperparts dull gray-brown, with no blue-green flecks; underparts white, may have pale gray wash on breast; bill and legs as in adult male

Nestling: altricial and downy; skin pale pink; down whitish; mouth pale yellow; gape flanges white

Tree Swallows nest in a wide variety of habitats, but prefer open grassy areas, often, but not necessarily, near water. They take readily to using artificial nesting cavities. With the exception of the Violet-green Swallow (Tachycineta thalassina), Tree Swallows are the first swallow to return to breeding areas in the spring, with males arriving in advance of females by about one week.

Egg-laying is initiated once sufficient insect prey are available for egg production by females. In the more southern parts of the range this may be in late April, or in early to mid May in more northern areas.

Winters to Honduras, Nicaragua, and c Costa Rica. Rare cowbird host. Forestry practice of removing standing dead trees has greatly reduced availability of natural nest sites; will use nest boxes.
