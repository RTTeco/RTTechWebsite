Sunflowers
     The common sunflowers is a native of Mexico and Peru, introduced into this country in the sixteenth century and now one of our most familiar garden plants.
     It is an annual herb, with a rough, hairy stem, 3 to 12 feet high, broad, coarsely toothed, rough leaves, 3 to 12 inches long, and circular heads of flowers, 3 to 6 inches wide in wild specimens and often a foot or more in cultivation. The flower-heads are composed of many small tubular flowers arranged compactly on a flattish disk.
     They are tall, hardy, annual or perennial herbs. Most are which are grown in gardens, being of easy cultivation in moderately good soil, and that useful plant of the kitchen garden.
     The genus Helianthus, being derived from helios (the sun) and anthos (a flower), has the same meaning as the English name Sunflower, which it is popularly supposed has been given these flowers from a supposition that they follow the sun by day, always turning toward its direct rays.  Every part of the sunflower may be utilized for some economic purpose.    The leaves form a cattle-food and the stems contain fiber which may be used successfully in making paper. The seed is rich in oil, which is said to approach more nearly to olive oil than any other vegetable oil known. The flowers contain yellow dye. 
     The oil can be used in candle and soap making. The residue after the oil is expressed forms an important cattle-food. This oil-cake is relished by sheep, pigs, pigeons, rabbits, and poultry. The seeds make excellent chicken feed and feeding fowls on bruised Sunflower seeds is well known to increase their laying power.
     You can also roast sunflower seeds for snacks too.
