Killdeer

Charadrius vociferus

Scientific classification: The killdeer belongs to the family Charadriidae of the order Charadriiformes. It is classified as Charadrius vociferus.

Description:  

  • Length: 8 inches
  • Medium-sized, long-winged shorebird
  • Short, fairly thick dark bill
  • Legs flesh-colored
  • Red eye ring
  • White forehead and white stripe behind eye
  • Brown face; black lores and upper borders to the white forehead and collar
  • White collar
  • Brown cap, back and wings
  • White breast and belly
  • Two black breast bands
  • White wing stripe at the bases of the flight feathers is visible in flight
  • Rust-red rump
  • Brown tail with black subterminal band, white terminal band and barred, white outer tail feathers
  • Tail extends beyond wing tips at rest
  • Sexes similar
  • Juveniles similar to adult

Voice: You will definitely hear this bird before you see it.  These birds are ground nesters and will feign injury to draw predators away from their nest.  They are prevalent on golf courses and  anywhere there is an open field.

Reproduction: The parent killdeer start sitting on the eggs to incubate them as soon as all the eggs have been laid. The killdeer embryos inside the first-laid three eggs do not start developing while the eggs are sitting out in the cold. But when they feel the warmth of the parent killdeer, all four killdeer embryos start developing at the same time.  So even though the first-laid egg spends a longer time in the shell than the last-laid, all the killdeer chicks have the same development period. It takes 24 to 28 days of incubating for the chicks to hatch.

Fun Facts:  Throughout North America, south to central Mexico, the West Indies, and the Pacific coast of South America. It is a hardy bird, wintering in much of its breeding range. The killdeer is about 23 to 28 cm (about 9 to 11 in) long, brown above and white below, with two black bands traversing its breast. It is a powerful flier and rapid runner. The preferred breeding habitat is fields, pastures, and freshwater shores, and even grassy vacant land within city limits. The nest is a shallow depression in the ground. The four eggs are pointed and heavily marked with dark brown and black against a cream or buff background color.
