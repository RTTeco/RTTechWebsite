(Populus tremuloides)

  

   The quaking aspen is in the Poplar Family, and are located mostly in North America. 

HABITAT & GROWTH

   Quaking Aspens can survive in a wide climatic range, with temperatures varying from -78 degrees in winter to over 100 degrees in summer.  They are found up to 10,000 foot elevation, where precipitation is as low as 7 inches, and the growing season barely 80 days long.  On the opposite extreme is deep winter snows and areas that get over 50 inches of moisture.

   Quaking Aspens grow very fast in warmer climates and on better soils, but the colder regions and sub-alpine climates they have a very slow growth.

   Quaking Aspens have relatively short lives.  They can be as little as 50 years, but 80 to 90 years is more common.  Trees over 100 years can be found as well.

REPRODUCTION

   The technique and success rate of vegetative production is horribly low, despite the ease at which the roots send up new sprouts and suckers.  The seeds are tiny fluffs of cotton that blow all around.  The tiny seeds attached to the floating tufts number in the millions per pound, and live such a short time once shed.  First seeds blow in and sprout, then its the root spreading that becomes the pockets and hillsides of new Aspen forests.

FACTS

   - The wood is soft and light, making it not a good choice for firewood. Woodworking projects are a popular use.

   - The paper production is the primary use for the wood.

   - Also known as the trembling aspen, because the leaves tremble with the slightest breeze..

   - The is a favorite food for the beaver.
