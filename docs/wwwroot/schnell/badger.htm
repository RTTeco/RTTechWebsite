The badger is a bear-like animal with a stocky three foot body, short tail and short but powerful legs that are armed with strong claws on the front feet.  It also walks on the soles of its feet like a bear, but the resemblance of the beaver to the bear ends there.  The badger is a member of the carnivore, or flesh eater, family.  The badger is in the family Mustelidae.  A characteristic of the Mustelidae family if that their foot prints show five toes.  This is a excellent way of distinguishing the foot prints of a beaver from those of a dog's footprints.  Dogs prints only show four toes.

    The American Badger bears a greater resemblance to the European Badger, but doesn't have the black and white striped head.  The American Badger is widespread in North America from south-western Canada, and south to central Mexico.

    The sett in which a badger lives in is a complex underground network of tunnels and chambers, often with many entrances at ground level.  The badger lines the chambers of the sett with bedding made up of dry bracken and grass or leaves.  It drags out old bedding frequently and replaces it with new bedding.  Being a very clean animal, the badger will scoop out a toilet for itself above ground.

    Badgers collect large amount of dry grass, straw bracken, or similar material to line the nest chamber in their communal underground sett.  At the end of winter, all of it is scraped out and dumped outside the sett.  This makes room for new bedding to be put in place.

   The badger eats a diet of small mammals, birds, reptiles, and arthropods.  They catch their food by digging.
