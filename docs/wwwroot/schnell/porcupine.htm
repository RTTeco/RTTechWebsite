North American Porcupine

 
     The North American Porcupine is a well protected, plant eating rodent that spends most of its time in trees, looking for food. These slow moving animals have sharp, needle like quills protecting their body. Porcupines live up to 20 years in captivity. They live in forests, deserts, and grasslands in much of North America. Their predators include the cougar, coyote, and bobcat. 
     This rodent usually weighs around 22 pounds. Its blunt-nosed face has small eyes, and small, round ears. The legs are powerful with long curved claws making it slow footed and strong. The front half of the body is covered in long guard hairs which are yellowish color in the west and black or brown in the east. The rump and tail are covered in over 30,000 quills which are 1-2 inches in length and set with tiny scale like barbs. The quills are mainly used for defense purposes.  
     North American Porcupine are herbivores who eat leaves, bark, evergreen needles, buds, twigs, fruit, and salt. Common porcupines are also known to gnaw on bones and antlers from the ground due to their high mineral content.
     Reproduction occurs in the fall or early winter. After  mating, the female repels the male and a copulation plug is formed. The gestation period lasts between 205 and 217 days with the young being born from April to June. Litters normally consist of single offspring. Offspring are born with their eyes wide open. The mothers remain with their young for up to six months .
     The common porcupine is primarily found in coniferous forests but are also frequently found in deciduous of mixed forests.
