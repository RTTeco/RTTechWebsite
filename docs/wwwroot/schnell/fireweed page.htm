Description:

Kochia is an annual forb growing from 2-5 feet tall and begins growth in late spring. The stems of kochia are erect, much-branched and leafy. Leaves are arranged alternately on the stems, which become bright red with age. Interest in the plant as a feed source has been high in recent years exhibiting crude protein levels of 12 to 25 percent. Kochia can produce the same amount of forage per acre as alfalfa with half the water; hence, the plant is often called "poor man's alfalfa".

Distribution and Habitat:

Kochia is an annual plant originating in Eurasia, and introduced into America in the early 1900's. Its appearance was first recorded in Texas during the late 1940's. It is found throughout most of the United States and occurs locally on disturbed sites and in old fields.

Toxic Agent:

The plant contains a number of agents which could contribute to its toxicity. These are oxalates, alkaloids, saponins, nitrate, and sulfate. Oxalate poisoning by kochia involves a mineral imbalance caused when calcium becomes bound by oxalates. Calcium levels drop in the blood and this results in sudden death. There may be liver damage in some animals which results in photosensitization. In other cases there may be polioencephalomalacia which could be due to the high sulfate.

Livestock Symptoms:

There are several symptoms characteristic of kochia poisoning depending on the toxic principle. Some general symptoms include:

  • nervousness or confusion
  • incoordination
  • muscular spasms
  • blindness
  • a tendency to lie down
  • sudden death

Potential syndromes from kochia poisonings include (see Descriptions of Animal Conditions for specific symptoms):

  • polioencephalomalacia
  • oxalate poisoning
  • photosensitization
  • liver damage
  • nitrate poisoning

Integrated Management Strategies:

Two treatments have been used with varying degrees of success to counteract oxalate poisoning from kochia. One of these is an intravenous injection of calcium gluconate, while the other is dicalcium phosphate fed free choice at 25 percent of the usual salt ration. The latter treatment is often used as a preventive measure when cattle or sheep are grazing pastures heavily infested with kochia. Caution must be used when cattle grazing kochia are allowed access to water containing high amounts of salt. Some ranchers have had success feeding free choice hay straw to their animals to reduce toxicity problems. In cases of polio, treat affected animals with vitamin B (thiamine).

Due to the annual growth habit, mechanical or chemical control treatments are effective when desired but are generally not warranted. It is generally recommended that cattle should not graze kochia for longer than approximately 60 days. Kochia can be considered a valuable forage in some circumstances.
