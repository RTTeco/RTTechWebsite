(falco mexicanus)

A swift and agile flyer, the Prairie Falcon ranges widely over plains, desert, open mountains, and canyons. Paler than its close relative, the Peregrine, the Prairie has the cheek marks and pointed wings and the sharp, curved talons and beak of the falcon family. (The name falcon comes from the Latin word falx which means "sickle", referring to the shape of the talons and beak.)

Often flying low to the ground to catch rodents and small birds, this bird can dive from heights at very high speeds, and because of its lighter body, fly even faster than the Peregrine.

While the Peregrine Falcon was severely affected in the past by the accumulating metallic poisons in the food-chain, derived from eating water fowl and causing egg-shell thinning, the Prairie has shown some of this effect from eating Horned Larks that have fed on newly-planted seeds which are treated with poison. A more direct adverse effect of agricultural activity is the reduction of habitat, especially hunting grounds.

In several falcons, including the Prairie Falcon the female is about 1/3 larger than the male. This is thought to be a way of reducing competition for food, especially in bird-hunting raptors - hence the males feed on smaller species of prey while the larger females go for bigger birds.

Prairie falcons nest in crevices or on ledges of cliffs; 4-5 eggs are laid and young are cared for after they have learned to fly.
