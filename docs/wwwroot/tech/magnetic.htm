For the more than 1 million people who make that journey every year by air, this may seem like a fantasy. But it isn't. Such is the prospect which lies before us early in the 21st century, if we implement some type of program for investment in magnetic levitation (Maglev) transport systems.
Here's what such a trip would be like. Passengers arrive at Boston's main station via a network of Magellan commuter lines, at more than 50 miles per hour, shortly before the scheduled 7:30 a.m. departure of the morning express service to Washington, D.C., perhaps to be known as The 21stCentury Unlimited. There are no worries about traffic jams or parking spaces. On board, passengers relax in quiet comfort, while The Unlimited accelerates at nearly three feet per second per second to a cruising speed of about 300 mph.
Following a re-engineered route from Boston to Providence, R.l., and then along the Connecticut coastal strip, (see map) the first, and only stop, New York City, would be at about 8:15 a.m. From there, The Unlimited would speed toward Washington, D.C., along roughly the same path Am-trak's Metro liner now follows, arriving at around 9:00 a.m., the beginning of the working day.

Magnetic levitation: A suspension and propulsion system for trains, based upon the operational principles of a magnet, also maglev train, a high speed ground transportation vehicle levitated above a track called a guide way and propelled by magnetic fields. The principles of these magnets were developed by the 18th century French scientist Charles Augustin De Coulomb. coulomb's law states: Like poles repel each other and unlike poles attract each other. The north and south poles of a magnet will pull together, while a north and a north or a south and a south pole will push away from each other. Maglev is short for magnetic levitation, a fancy way of saying floating on magnets. A great deal of research as been developed to produce super conducting materials. Super conducting materials are those that produce little or no resistance to the flow of electricity . These advancements are leading the way to improved and highly efficient magnets. These magnets can then be used to levitate or raise vehicles on a magnetic field. Instead of pounding along on tires or rails, the vehicle floats several inches above its guide way on a cushion of magnetism. By reducing the friction made when two materials rub against each other this will make the vehicle much more efficient. As the development of super conducting material improves, magnetic levitation will certainly become more feasible. Magnetic levitation train technology can be used for urban travel at relatively low speeds (100km/h or 62 mph). A short distance maglev shuttle has been operating since 1984 between Birmingham, England, airport and the city train station. However, the greatest world wide interest is in high speed maglev systems. Train speeds of 517 km/h (321 mph) have been demonstrated by a full size maglev vehicle in Japan, while in Germany a maglev train has run at 435 km/h (270 mph). Maglev Systems Two different approaches to magnetic levitation train systems have been developed. Those in which the systems are onboard the vehicles, such as Japan's HSST (High Speed Super Transport) models, and those which are propelled and controlled from the track on which the vehicles run, known as the guide way. Both the German TR-07 and the Japanese MLU-002 models make use of what are called passive systems. However, the German and Japanese programs make use of different electromagnetic principles to provide the suspension, propulsion, and guidance of their vehicles. The first, called electromagnetic suspension (EMS), uses conventional electromagnets mounted at the ends of a pair of structures under the train; the structures wrap around and under either side of the guide way. The magnets attract up toward laminated iron rails in the guide way and lift the train. However, this system is inherently unstable; the distance between the electromagnets and the guide way, which is about 10 mm (3/8), must be continuously monitored and adjusted by computer to prevent the train from hitting the guide way. The German Tran rapid is based on the attractive power of these magnetic forces, (Figure 1). The vehicle's under frame "wraps around" the guide way and pushes the vehicle up and off its rails. The second design, called electrodynamics suspension (EDS), uses the opposing force between super conducting magnets on the vehicle and electrically conductive strips or coils in the guide way to levitate the train. This approach is stable and does not require the continued monitoring that the EMS system needs. In the EDS system the distance between the guide way and the train are greater
(4 to 6 inches). However the EDS trains use super conducting magnets, which are more expensive than conventional electromagnets and they also require refrigeration to keep the magnets at a very low temperature. The Japanese also make use of repulsive forces in the electrodynamics Suspension system (Figure 2), to lift the vehicle away from the guide way. These systems must employ an undercarriage-like landing-gear, for lift-off and landing, because the vehicles only levitate at speeds in excess of 25 mph. Magnetic levitation trains in Germany and Japan are capable of reaching speeds up to 300 miles per hour. Maglev systems offer a number of advantages over conventional trains that use steel wheels on steel rails. Because magnetic levitation trains do not touch the guide way, maglev systems over come the principle limitation of wheeled trains; the high cost of maintaining precise alignment of the tracks to avoid excessive vibration and rail deterioration at high speeds. Maglevs can provide sustained speeds greater than 500 km/h (300 mph), limited only by the cost of power to overcome wind resistance. The fact that maglevs do not touch the guide way also has other advantages: faster acceleration and braking; greater climbing capability; enhanced operation in heavy rain, snow, and ice. Maglev transportation would be a solution to mass transit problems in major metropolitan areas where traffic on ground and air have become major problems. The major appeals of the maglev solution is its high speed, safety, environmentally clean and low noise. Maglev systems are also energy efficient; for distances of several hundred miles, they use about half the energy per passenger as a typical commercial aircraft. Electrified transportation systems, such as maglev systems, also the use of petroleum, and pollute the air less than aircraft, diesel locomotives, and automobiles. Implementing a maglev system will create thousands of jobs for high level scientists, engineers, technicians and managers, as well as construction crews and laborers across the country. Maglev technology was pioneered on Long Island New York in the 1960's by two Brookhaven National Laboratory scientists, James Powell and Gordon Danby, under a Federal Railroad Administration program sponsored under the High Speed Ground Transportation Act of 1965. who used super conducting magnets for maglev trains. This was recognized world wide as a break through because of the super magnets power to lift enormous weights. The linear electric motor, the power source for all current maglev prototypes, In 1971, contracts were awarded the Ford Motor Company and Stanford Research Institute for experimental development of maglev power sources. Low-speed propulsion systems for cities were advanced by Rohr Industries, with Boeing taking up the development rights. In 1972, Henry Kolm, of Magneplane International and Richard Thorton of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology built a magneplane concept featuring repulsive super conducting magnet with a semi-circular guide way that permits self banking suspension without a secondary suspension system. In 1974, a world speed record of 255.4 mph was set by a prototype linear induction motor vehicle at the Department of Transportation's Pueblo, Colorado test facility. Just one year later, in 1975, federal funding for the program was cut, when the Ford administration and Congress allowed the 1965 act to lapse. In Germany and Japan High-speed maglev transportation systems (HSST) are already being built in Germany and Japan. In Germany, the Tran rapid system has received government approval for commercial operations. Its first phase will connect the airports of Cologne-Bonn and Düsseldorf, and later Essen. The Tran rapid soon expects approval for mainline, intercity operations. This program is about seven years ahead of the maglev program in Japan, where the MLU system is scheduled to begin transporting passengers in the densely populated 320 square mile Tokyo-Nagoya-Osaka corridor by the end of the decade. Germany and Japan have also developed systems such as the M-Bahn, the 50 mph magnetically levitated urban transit system, which functions in Berlin, and the HSST Corporation's systems, which have provided vehicles for exposition sites in Tsukba and Yokohama in Japan, and Vancouver, British Columbia, in Canada. Maglev technology is already set to meet a family of transportation functions, from short-distance, but relative high-speed urban commutes-Japan's HSST can function at between 60 and 250 mph-to intercity travel at speeds in excess of 310 mph. The Transrapid TR-07 is capable of carrying up to 200 passengers at speeds of up to 310 mph. With a one-minute headway between units, Germany's TR-07 can transport between 10,000 and 20,000 people per hour. Japan's commercial design maglev train will consist of 14 cars capable of carrying 900 passengers, and is intended to move 75 -100,000 people per day between Tokyo and Osaka. Maglev is set to revolutionize passenger and freight transportation worldwide by early in the next century, just as the steam engine revolutionized transportation more than 150 years ago. Given the spin-off's which will follow the development of the transportation systems themselves, such as impetus given to so-called "high-temperature" superconductor scientific research, the effect will be even more profound. Current plans for high speed maglev systems include a 283 km (175 mile) Berlin to Hamburg route in Germany. This has been approved by the German parliament to begin in the year 2005. In Japan, a 43 km (27 mile) maglev test track in under construction in Yamanashi Prefecture, about 100 km (62 miles) west of Tokyo. After tests on the latest maglev vehicle are completed, the test track is planned to be extended to Tokyo and Osaka. This new commercial line will relieve passenger demand on the Shinkansen high speed train that currently operates at peak speeds of 225 km (140 mph). Currently a consortium of four major United States companies, Grumman, Bechtel, Foster-Miller and Magneplane, are submitting proposals for a United States model. Much of the interest in maglev systems has been initiated at the state level with plans developed in Florida, California, Nevada, New York, and Pennsylvania. Studies completed by the federal government in 1993 conclude that the potential benefits of a United States maglev system justify staring a national development program. Although there are no technical barriers to installing a maglev system, research and development is needed to refine the technology and determine how to reduce costs. In addition, for maglevs to become a part of the nations transportation system, they will have to be viewed as one of the necessary parts of an integrated transportation infrastructure, like highways and airports. THE MAGLEV VEHICLE

You and your partner will be constructing one maglev vehicle as a team or you may each construct one for yourself but you do need to work together during this part of your rotation. There are five categories that you may choose to enter. Read through each of the categories and choose which type of vehicle you would like to construct. After completion, you will make a video tape of your maglev vehicle test run. You may keep your vehicle that you construct but will have to return batteries, motors, propellers etc., unless you supply your own materials. There is plenty of time in this module for you or your team member to make more than one maglev model. It is suggested that you begin with the gravity powered vehicle, test it and then construct one of the more difficult models.

The specifications of the maglev vehicle for all categories are:
Width of vehicle = 2 1/2 inches
Minimum length of vehicle = 3 1/2 inches
Maximum length of vehicle = 6 inches
Vehicle must have a passenger compartment or body at least 3 cubic inches.
The vehicle must operate free of human control or interference other than initial set-up and release.
Any materials are acceptable for construction including cardboard, paper, foam board, plastics, balsa. Materials can be gathered from  the resource boxes or be brought from home. Your performance  grade will depend on your creativity. It will be up to you to experiment  with a variety of materials.
Vehicle must attach to the maglev test platform with tape provided by  the teacher. You will be allowed three test runs to acquire a time for the fastest  vehicle.
Restrictions for the maglev vehicle for all categories are:   
Sling shot or push vehicles are not allowed   
No CO-2 cartridges, compressed air, rocket engines or model airplane engines are not allowed.    
System must operate free of human control or interference  other than initial set-up and release.

Category One: Gravity Power
For this test, the vehicle must be able to traverse the 8 foot track when the track is elevated a maximum of 24 inches on one end. Speed is not important. In this category there is a weight limit of 100 grams or 3 1/2 ounces. You will score higher if your vehicle is able to traverse the track at a minimum elevated height.

Category Two: Wind Power  For this test, the power will be provided by a 4 inch fan. The fan will be located at the beginning of the track and stand on the same level as the track. The track will be elevated a maximum of 5 inches on one end. You should try to develop a vehicle that will not only traverse the 8 foot track but also do it with the track elevated a minimum or not elevated at all. No weight restrictions apply to this category. Operate the fan at a voltage of no greater than 4 DCV.

Category Three: Self Contained - Balloon Power For this test, the power will be provided by a balloon provided by the teacher. The balloon must be attached to the vehicle and have no connections off the vehicle. The track will be elevated a minimum of 5 inches or less on one end. You should try to develop a vehicle that will not only traverse the 8 foot track but also do it in the fastest time and with a minimum of height on one end. No weight restrictions apply to this category. The most difficult part of this design is developing a nozzle for the balloon.

Category Four: Self Contained - Rubber band Power For this test, the power will be provided by a standard rubber band provided by the teacher. The track will be elevated approximately 5 inches or less on one end. You should try to develop a vehicle that will not only traverse the 8 foot track but also do it in the fastest time, and with a minimum of height on one end. No weight restrictions apply to this category.

Category Five: Self Contained - Battery Power For this test, the power will be provided by one to four AA batteries (6 volts) and a small motor provided by the teacher. The track will be elevated approximately 5 inches or less on one end. You should try to develop a vehicle that will not only traverse the 8 foot track but also do it in the fastest time, and with a minimum of height on one end. No weight restrictions apply to this category. The track can be elevated a minimum of height and should be noted on your results. Category Six: Electrified Track For this test, the power will be provided by a power supply (6 volts) that will be connected to the track. The track will be elevated approximately 5 inches or less on one end. You should try to develop a vehicle that will not only traverse the 8 foot track but also do it in the fastest time, and with a minimum of height on one end. No weight restrictions apply to this category. The most difficult part of this design is developing the connection between the vehicle and the track.
